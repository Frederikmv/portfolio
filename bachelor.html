UNDERSTANDING THE HOME: A TAKE ON ARTIFACT ECOLOGIES, ROLES AND SHAREABILITY

Abstract

This bachelor thesis examines roles and shareability in terms of technology use in the household as well as how they affect artifact ecologies of family members. Studies regarding artifact ecology as of far have mostly focused on personal and private artifacts and how they affect the one individual, however, a limited amount of research have been focused on how artifacts can be intertwined between different individuals' ecologies within households. Furthermore, limited amount of research have been conducted with regard to roles and shareability in the home. A preliminary ethnographic study was conducted in four households with the aim of creating floor plans for better understanding the location and shareability of technologies. Furthermore, a follow-up study was conducted to confirm our floor plans as well as further questions. With the results from the studies six roles were suggested describing the responsibilities regarding technologies in the household. To better understand whether an artifact is personal or shared in the complex setting of a household and what roles different family members takes upon themselves, two models were developed based on the findings from the two user studies. This bachelor aims to aid designers and researchers alike better understand the complex setting of a household by contributing with the tools necessary to analyze roles and shareability.

Roles and dynamics in households

We identified six common roles defined by individuals technology capabilities in the home as a result of our user studies. The roles are dynami which means that a family member is not confined to one role but can have several roles as well as temporarily step into other roles depending on the context
